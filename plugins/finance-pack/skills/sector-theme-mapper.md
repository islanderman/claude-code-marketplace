# Sector Theme Mapper

## ULTRATHINK Activation

**Mode**: STRATEGIST MODE with EXPERT MODE -- institutional-grade thematic investment analysis
**Optimization**: Systematic value chain decomposition with breadth-first player identification and rigorous TAM validation
**Focus**: Converting broad investment themes into specific, actionable positions with quantified exposure and evidence-based conviction

---

## Purpose

Transform broad investment themes (AI, energy transition, aging population) into structured, actionable investment maps. This skill decomposes themes into value chain segments, categorizes every relevant player by market cap tier and pure-play exposure, reality-checks inflated TAM estimates, and separates consensus crowded trades from under-the-radar opportunities. The output is a prioritized shortlist with specific tickers and investment theses.

**When to use**: When a macro or secular theme is identified and needs to be translated into specific portfolio positions, when evaluating whether a thematic ETF captures the right exposures, or when screening for overlooked beneficiaries of a structural shift.

---

## Methodology (4-Phase)

### Phase 1: Analysis -- Theme Decomposition and Value Chain Mapping

Analyze with deliberation to decompose the broad theme into discrete sub-themes and value chain segments. Use step-by-step reasoning to map the full chain from upstream inputs through midstream infrastructure to downstream end-users. Identify where value accrues at each stage. Evaluate assumptions about which segments will capture the most economic value -- the obvious answer is often wrong (picks-and-shovels narratives are not always correct).

### Phase 2: Plan -- Player Universe Construction

Apply breadth-first reasoning to build a comprehensive player universe across all market cap tiers. For each player, score pure-play exposure (what percentage of revenue comes from this theme?). Classify each player as consensus (widely recognized thematic play) or under-the-radar (overlooked or misunderstood exposure). Map competitive dynamics within each value chain segment -- is this winner-take-most or fragmented?

### Phase 3: Execution -- TAM Validation and Risk Assessment

Apply CRITIC MODE to reality-check TAM estimates. Most thematic TAMs are inflated by 2-5x by sell-side analysts and industry associations with incentive to overstate. Perform bottom-up TAM calculation alongside top-down. Flag adoption curve stage (innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority). Assess theme-specific risks across regulatory, technology, adoption, and competition dimensions. Use multi-layer analysis to identify second-order effects and indirect beneficiaries.

### Phase 4: Validation -- Prioritization and Thesis Construction

Self-check the complete analysis by verifying internal consistency. Do pure-play scores match actual revenue breakdowns? Are "under-the-radar" picks genuinely overlooked or just poor businesses? Does the TAM reality-check support the growth assumptions embedded in current valuations? Produce a prioritized Top 3 picks with specific tickers and concise investment theses. Verify logic: Would you put real capital behind these picks?

## Detailed Framework

### Theme Decomposition

Break the broad theme into 3-5 sub-themes, each representing a distinct investment angle:

```
Theme: [Broad Theme Name]
|

|   Why it matters: [Value creation mechanism]
|
+-- Sub-Theme 2: [Name] -- [1-sentence description]
|   Why it matters: [Value creation mechanism]
|
+-- Sub-Theme 3: [Name] -- [1-sentence description]
|   Why it matters: [Value creation mechanism]
```

### Value Chain Mapping

Map the complete value chain with key players at each stage:

```
UPSTREAM (Inputs/Components)
  [Segment A]: [Player 1], [Player 2], [Player 3]
  [Segment B]: [Player 1], [Player 2]
       |
       v
MIDSTREAM (Infrastructure/Platforms)
  [Segment C]: [Player 1], [Player 2], [Player 3]
  [Segment D]: [Player 1], [Player 2]
       |
       v
DOWNSTREAM (Applications/End Users)
  [Segment E]: [Player 1], [Player 2], [Player 3]
  [Segment F]: [Player 1], [Player 2]
```

For each value chain segment, assess:
- **Value capture**: What % of theme economics flows to this segment?
- **Competitive structure**: Monopoly / Oligopoly / Fragmented?
- **Margin profile**: High gross margin (>60%) / Moderate (30-60%) / Low (<30%)?

### Player Universe by Market Cap Tier

Categorize all identified players into tiers. Each tier offers different risk/reward profiles:

| Tier | Market Cap Range | Characteristics | Typical Role in Theme |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Mega Cap** | >$200B | Low pure-play exposure, theme is small % of total, lowest risk | Platform/infrastructure providers, acquirers |
| **Large Cap** | $10-200B | Moderate exposure, established operations, lower risk | Category leaders, scaled operators |
| **Mid Cap** | $2-10B | Higher exposure, growth phase, moderate risk | Focused players, potential acquisition targets |
| **Small Cap** | $300M-2B | High pure-play exposure, early stage, higher risk | Specialists, first-movers, niche leaders |
| **Micro Cap** | <$300M | Maximum exposure but maximum risk, liquidity concerns | Pre-revenue, unproven, speculative |

### Pure-Play Scoring

Score each player 1-10 on thematic exposure. This is the single most important metric for thematic investing -- a company that is "in the theme" at 5% of revenue is not a thematic play.

| Score | Revenue Exposure | Classification |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| 9-10 | >80% of revenue from theme | **Pure Play** -- stock moves almost entirely with theme |
| 7-8 | 50-80% of revenue from theme | **High Exposure** -- significant thematic driver with some diversification |
| 5-6 | 25-50% of revenue from theme | **Moderate Exposure** -- theme is material but not dominant |
| 3-4 | 10-25% of revenue from theme | **Low Exposure** -- theme is a growth option, not core business |
| 1-2 | <10% of revenue from theme | **Negligible** -- marketing narrative exceeds actual exposure |

**Critical check**: Many companies are marketed as thematic plays with 1-2 score exposure. Verify actual revenue contribution, not press release claims.

### TAM Reality Check

Most thematic TAMs are inflated. Apply systematic validation:

**Top-Down TAM** (industry reports, sell-side estimates):
- Source: [Who produced this TAM?]
- Estimate: $XXB by 20XX
- Incentive bias: [Does the source benefit from an inflated TAM?]

**Bottom-Up TAM** (units x price, customers x ARPU):
- Addressable customers: [How many potential buyers exist?]
- Realistic penetration: [What % will actually adopt in timeframe?]
- Price point: [What will they actually pay?]
- Bottom-up estimate: $XXB

**Reality-check ratio**: Bottom-Up / Top-Down. If ratio is <0.5, the top-down TAM is likely inflated.

**Adoption Curve Stage Assessment**:

| Stage | Penetration | Characteristics | Investment Implication |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Innovators** | <5% | Unproven technology, regulatory unclear | Very high risk, venture-stage thesis |
| **Early Adopters** | 5-15% | Product-market fit proven, early commercial traction | High risk but asymmetric upside potential |
| **Early Majority** | 15-40% | Rapid scaling, unit economics proven, competition intensifying | Sweet spot for growth investing |
| **Late Majority** | 40-70% | Mainstream adoption, growth decelerating, consolidation | Transition to value metrics |
| **Laggards** | >70% | Saturated, GDP-like growth, dividend focus | Yield play, not growth play |

**Inflation Flags** (red flags that TAM is overstated):
- TAM includes adjacent markets that require separate products to address
- Assumes 80%+ penetration for a product category that historically maxes at 30-40%
- Uses total spending in a category, not the addressable portion that could shift to new solution
- Conflates global TAM with serviceable addressable market (SAM)
- Growth rate implies market size doubling in 3 years without historical precedent in the category

### Consensus vs Under-the-Radar Classification

| Category | Criteria | Implication |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Consensus Overweight** | Widely held by thematic ETFs, featured in sell-side research, high analyst coverage (>20) | Likely fairly priced or expensive. Upside requires outperformance vs already-high expectations. |
| **Consensus Recognized** | Known thematic play, moderate analyst coverage (10-20), held by some thematic funds | Moderate alpha opportunity. Market is aware but may not fully price in execution. |
| **Under-the-Radar** | Low analyst coverage (<10), not in major thematic ETFs, thematic connection not widely discussed | Higher alpha potential if thesis is correct. But verify: is it overlooked or just a bad business? |
| **Misunderstood** | Classified in wrong sector by market, thematic exposure hidden in segment reporting | Highest alpha potential. Re-rating catalyst when market discovers the exposure. |

### Winner-Take-Most vs Fragmented Assessment

For each value chain segment, assess market structure:

- **Winner-Take-Most**: Strong network effects, high switching costs, economies of scale. 1-2 players will capture 60%+ share. **Strategy**: Identify and own the likely winner.
- **Oligopoly**: 3-5 players share the market with defensible positions. **Strategy**: Own the best-positioned 1-2, avoid laggards.
- **Fragmented**: No clear structural advantages, many competitors. **Strategy**: Be cautious with individual names; consider ETF or avoid.

## Quality Criteria

- Theme decomposed into distinct sub-themes (not just restating the broad theme)
- Value chain includes at least 3 segments with specific players at each level
- Pure-play scores verified against actual revenue data (not marketing claims)
- TAM includes both top-down and bottom-up with reality-check ratio
- Under-the-radar picks genuinely overlooked (not just small/bad companies)
- Top 3 picks include tickers, specific theses, and are internally consistent with the analysis
- Risk assessment covers all 4 dimensions with theme-specific (not generic) risks
- Self-check: Are any "pure plays" actually low-exposure companies riding a narrative?

---

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Picks-and-shovels fallacy**: Assuming upstream suppliers always win. Sometimes they are commoditized and capture minimal value (memory chip makers during the PC revolution). Verify that the "picks-and-shovels" segment has structural advantages, not just thematic proximity.

2. **TAM credulity**: Accepting industry-sponsored TAM estimates at face value. A 2024 sell-side report claiming $500B AI software TAM by 2027 deserves the same scrutiny as a 2000 report claiming $1T e-commerce by 2005. Always build bottom-up.

3. **Consensus-as-alpha confusion**: Recommending NVIDIA for AI or Tesla for EV as if this is insight. Consensus picks may be good companies but offer limited alpha -- the theme is priced in. Thematic mapping should surface at least one non-obvious opportunity.

4. **Pure-play score inflation**: Giving high scores to mega-caps with <10% thematic revenue exposure. Microsoft is a great company but scores 3/10 as an "AI pure play" -- AI is material but not dominant. Score exposure honestly.

5. **Ignoring adoption curve stage**: Treating all themes as early-stage growth stories. Some themes (cloud computing, e-commerce) are in late majority. Applying early-adopter investment frameworks to late-majority themes leads to overpaying for deceleration.

## Examples

### Example 1: AI Infrastructure Theme Mapping

**Theme decomposition**:
- Sub-Theme 1: **AI Compute Hardware** -- Chips and systems purpose-built for AI training and inference. Value creation through compute demand outstripping supply.
- Sub-Theme 2: **AI Cloud Infrastructure** -- Hyperscale data centers and cloud platforms providing AI-as-a-service. Value creation through massive CapEx cycle and consumption-based revenue.
- Sub-Theme 3: **AI Data Infrastructure** -- Data storage, processing, and pipeline tools feeding AI models. Value creation through exponential data growth and data quality requirements.
- Sub-Theme 4: **AI Model / Platform Layer** -- Foundation model providers and AI development platforms. Value creation through platform lock-in and API monetization.

**Value chain** (abbreviated):
- Upstream: Semiconductor equipment (ASML, LRCX) -> Chip design (NVDA, AMD, AVGO) -> Memory (MU, SK Hynix)
- Midstream: Cloud/data centers (AMZN/AWS, MSFT/Azure, GOOGL/GCP) -> Networking (ANET, CSCO)
- Downstream: AI platforms (MSFT/OpenAI, GOOGL, META) -> Enterprise AI software (CRM, PLTR, SNOW)

**TAM reality check**: Top-down AI infrastructure TAM estimates range $200-500B by 2028. Bottom-up: ~$150B cloud AI spend + ~$50B on-prem AI hardware = ~$200B serviceable. Reality-check ratio: 0.40-1.0x. Lower-end estimates are plausible; upper-end is inflated. Adoption stage: **Early Majority** (15-40% enterprise penetration for AI workloads).

**Top 3 picks**:
1. **AVGO** -- Broadcom ($175, $800B). Sub-theme: AI Compute. Pure-play: 5/10 (AI ~35% of revenue via custom ASICs and networking). Thesis: Best-positioned custom silicon provider for hyperscaler AI CapEx; networking portfolio creates dual exposure to data center buildout. Classification: **Consensus Recognized** but custom ASIC angle underappreciated vs NVDA narrative.
2. **ANET** -- Arista Networks ($340, $105B). Sub-theme: AI Cloud Infrastructure. Pure-play: 7/10 (>60% revenue from cloud/AI networking). Thesis: Every AI cluster requires networking infrastructure; Arista dominates high-performance cloud networking with 800G transition providing multi-year upgrade cycle. Classification: **Consensus Recognized** transitioning to **Consensus Overweight**.
3. **VRT** -- Vertiv Holdings ($105, $40B). Sub-theme: AI Cloud Infrastructure. Pure-play: 6/10 (~50% revenue from data center power/cooling). Thesis: AI data center power density is 3-5x traditional; Vertiv supplies critical thermal management and power distribution that every AI buildout requires. Classification: **Under-the-Radar** -- not in most AI ETFs, classified as Industrial, not Tech.

### Example 2: Weight Loss Drug / GLP-1 Theme Mapping

**Theme decomposition**:
- Sub-Theme 1: **GLP-1 Drug Manufacturers** -- Companies producing semaglutide, tirzepatide, and next-gen compounds. Value creation through blockbuster drug revenue.
- Sub-Theme 2: **GLP-1 Supply Chain** -- CDMOs, API suppliers, and device manufacturers enabling scale-up. Value creation through manufacturing bottleneck resolution.
- Sub-Theme 3: **GLP-1 Losers** -- Industries facing demand destruction as obesity rates decline. Value creation through short/underweight positioning.
- Sub-Theme 4: **GLP-1 Adjacent Beneficiaries** -- Companies benefiting from healthier populations or increased GLP-1 adoption infrastructure.

**Value chain**:
- Upstream: API/CDMO (Catalent/CTLT, Samsung Biologics) -> Drug delivery devices (BFAM, West Pharma/WST)
- Midstream: Drug manufacturers (LLY, NVO, AMGN) -> PBMs/Distributors (CVS/Caremark, CI/Evernorth, MCK)
- Downstream: Telehealth prescribers (HIMS, AMWL) -> Pharmacies (WBA, CVS)
- Negative downstream: Bariatric surgery (ISRG partially), medical devices for obesity comorbidities (MDT sleep apnea, ABT diabetes), processed food (KHC, GIS)

**TAM reality check**: Top-down GLP-1 market estimates: $100-150B by 2030. Bottom-up: ~80M US adults clinically eligible, ~15-20% realistic adoption (insurance + price barriers), ~$5,000/yr avg net price = $60-80B US + ~$30B ex-US = $90-110B. Reality-check ratio: 0.67-0.73x. Adoption stage: **Early Adopters** (estimated 5-8% of eligible population currently on GLP-1s).

**Top 3 picks**:
1. **LLY** -- Eli Lilly ($780, $740B). Sub-theme: GLP-1 Manufacturers. Pure-play: 6/10 (tirzepatide growing to ~40%+ of revenue). Thesis: Mounjaro/Zepbound plus next-gen oral and combination compounds create deepest GLP-1 pipeline; superior weight loss efficacy data vs competition supports premium pricing. Classification: **Consensus Overweight** -- fully recognized, premium valuation reflects consensus.
2. **HIMS** -- Hims & Hers Health ($22, $4.5B). Sub-theme: GLP-1 Adjacent. Pure-play: 4/10 (GLP-1 prescriptions growing rapidly but still minority of revenue). Thesis: Telehealth platform capturing GLP-1 demand from patients unwilling to navigate traditional healthcare; compounding pharmacy angle provides margin advantage while branded drugs are supply-constrained. Classification: **Under-the-Radar** -- market views as telehealth/wellness, not GLP-1 play. Key risk: FDA compounding crackdown if branded supply normalizes.
3. **Short KHC** -- Kraft Heinz ($33, $40B). Sub-theme: GLP-1 Losers. Pure-play: N/A (negative exposure). Thesis: Ultra-processed food portfolio faces secular demand headwind as GLP-1 adoption reduces caloric intake; already weak brand portfolio becomes structurally impaired if 15-20% of obese adults reduce consumption by 25-30%. Classification: **Under-the-Radar** as a short -- market has not priced in GLP-1 demand destruction for processed food.
